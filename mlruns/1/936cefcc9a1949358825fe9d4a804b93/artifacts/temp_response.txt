The product life cycle is a process that outlines how a product develops and changes over time, from its conception through to eventual retirement or obsolescence. Typically, a new product is developed in the design stage, where it is created using the latest technology and materials. This phase may involve conducting market research and gathering feedback to determine what users want and need.

Next, the product moves into its developmental stage, which involves refining the design and adding features that will make it more appealing to consumers. These changes are typically driven by consumer needs or market demands. The developmental stage can last anywhere from a few months to several years depending on the complexity of the product and its target audience.

Once the product has been developed, it enters the manufacturing stage. This phase involves the creation of the actual product, which is usually produced using traditional methods such as injection molding or extrusion casting. The manufacturing process typically involves testing the product's quality and reliability to ensure that it meets industry standards.

After manufacturing, the product enters the assembly stage. During this phase, various components are assembled together into a functional unit. This phase can also involve testing again to ensure that each component functions properly as intended.

Finally, the product enters the marketing and sales stage. In this phase, it is promoted through various channels, including advertising, social media, and word-of-mouth advertising. The goal of this phase is to generate interest and demand for the product among potential customers.

Over time, as a product ages or becomes outdated, it may be retired from the market, which can lead to obsolescence. Obsolescence typically occurs when the product's features become outdated or its manufacturer no longer produces new products that offer similar features. Retiring a product from the market is usually due to the product becoming too costly or technically unfeasible to produce, or if it has lost relevance in the marketplace.

In summary, the product life cycle is a process that outlines how a product develops and changes over time, ultimately leading to its eventual retirement or obsolescence.
